Making the Invisible Child Visible

Invisible Child Story Infographic

On December 9, 2013, the New York Times Published a series of articles about Dasani, one of the 20,000 Homeless Children living in New York. At 28,738 words, the story was in need of a visual summary to give an easier access point to the in-depth article.

Research

With 28,738 words about one of the most complex, politically charged issues in society, how could the Invisible Child Articles possibly be represented in a single graphic?

Focus on Dasani's story, not abstract charts and data

There are no shortage of statistics about poverty and homelessness, we are barraged with them constantly in attempts to change our views about issues. But numbers don't appeal to our emotions, we can't connect with a graph about poverty levels and then disengage. So instead of filling a poster with numbers and graphs, I wanted to show a slice of life for the girl struggling with so much. The first step was to collect as many notable facts about dasani as possible.

Ideation

Using sketches to visually think through the massive story

With over 100 index cards of information about Dasani's life, and the politics of poverty, I began to sketch different concepts to show her story visually.

Some different approaches quickly surfaced as I sketched, some focusing on the circles of influence people had in Dasani's life, the geography of her life, as well as the notable aspects of Dasani's personality.

I could either focus on the large scale forces affecting Dasani's life, or focus specifically on what one girl's experience of homelessness looks like.

Next, I began working at scale to see what type of visual would best tell Dasani's story in one glance.

Refinement

With a focus on telling Dasani's individual experience of homelessness, I began to take what seemed like the natural next step, create a digital version.

After a few digital iterations, there was something missing in the visualization. It lacked the impact and immediacy of the rough sketches I had been working with earlier in the project.

Dasani's story didn't need a pixel perfect digital infographic, it needed a visual story that reflected the struggle and messiness of her life.

With that in mind, I went back to the drawing board (literally) to find a way to integrate hand drawn elements into this visual story.

Gritty, yet full of personality, I used illustrations to communicate the different aspects of Dasani's life, in this case, her family. I created illustrations for the shelter, her school, her neighborhood, and a day in her life. Next, I had to find a way to integrate all of these illustrations together in a coherent way.

Using a restrained color pallete, gritty textures, and a typeface with an impactful presence and a slightly irregular look, Dasani's visual story had all of the elements it needed.

Bringing it All Together

Laying out the elements in a style similar to a comic book, I gradually added all of the illustrations, text, texture and digital color to complete the story. The process was a testament to the value of not letting the digital tools we use as designers dictate the outcome of our projects.
